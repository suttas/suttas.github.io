SN 47.8. The Cook

Mendicants, imagine a bad, foolish, and inexperienced cook attending to a king or a king’s adviser. He serves him various kinds of curries: sour, bitter, hot, sweet, sharp, mild, spicy, and bland ones. But he fails to notice the signs of his master. He does not notice which curry pleased his master on a given day, which one he reached for, which one he ate lots of, or which one he praised. That cook would not gain clothes, wages, and gifts. Why? Because that bad, foolish, and inexperienced cook fails to notice the signs of his master.

Likewise, some bad, foolish, and inexperienced mendicant might meditate on a certain aspect of the body, a certain sensation, a certain aspect of the mind, or a certain phenomenon—motivated, aware, and non-‍forgetful, free from longing and aversion for worldly things. As he does so, his mind does not unify, his impurities are not abandoned, and he fails to notice the signs of that. That mendicant does not gain blissful abidings in this very life, and he does not gain non-‍forgetfulness and awareness. Why? Because that bad, foolish, and inexperienced mendicant fails to notice the signs of own his mind.

Imagine a good, wise, and experienced cook attending to a king or a king’s adviser. He serves him various kinds of curries: sour, bitter, hot, sweet, sharp, mild, spicy, and bland ones. And he does notice the signs of his master. He notices which curry pleased his master on a given day, which one he reached for, which one he ate lots of, or which one he praised. That cook will gain clothes, wages, or gifts. Why? Because that good, wise, and experienced cook notices the signs of his own master.

Likewise, some good, wise, and experienced mendicant might meditate on a certain aspect of the body, a certain sensation, a certain aspect of the mind, or a certain phenomenon—motivated, aware, and non-‍forgetful, free from longing and aversion for worldly things. As he does so, his mind unifies, his impurities are abandoned, and he notices the signs of that. That mendicant does gain blissful abidings in this very life, and he gains non-‍forgetfulness and awareness. Why? Because that good, wise, experienced mendicant notices the signs of his own mind.
